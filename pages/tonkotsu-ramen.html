Tonkotsu Ramen

Description:

This is widely considered the "King of Ramen," hailing from Hakata, Fukuoka. The defining
characteristic is its cloudy, milky-white broth, achieved not by adding cream, but by boiling pork
bones for hours until the collagen, fat, and marrow emulsify into a rich, sticky soup.

Ingredients:

The Broth (The Heart of the Dish)

  • 4-5 lbs Pork trotters (pig's feet) and femurs (leg bones), cut to expose marrow.
  • 1 lb Pork fatback (optional, for extra richness).
  • 1 large Onion, unpeeled and halved.
  • 1 head Garlic, halved horizontally.
  • 3-inch Ginger root, sliced.
  • Water: Enough to cover the bones constantly.

The Seasoning (Shoyu Tare)

  • ½ cup Soy sauce.
  • 2 tbsp Mirin.
  • 1 piece Kombu (dried kelp), approx. 4x4 inches.
  • 1 tbsp Salt (adjust to taste).

The Essentials

  • Noodles: Fresh ramen noodles (thin, straight noodles are traditional for Tonkotsu).
  • Toppings: Chashu (braised pork belly), Ajitsuke Tamago (marinated soft-boiled egg),
    Menma (bamboo shoots), wood ear mushrooms (kikurage), and finely chopped scallions.

Steps:

  1. Blanch and Clean Bones

    This is the most critical step for a white broth.

    • Place the pork bones and trotters in a large stockpot and cover with cold water.
    • Bring to a boil and let it boil vigorously for 10–15 minutes. You will see grey/brown scum rise to the top.
    • Drain the water and scrub every bone thoroughly under cold running water. Remove any
      black bits or coagulated blood. The bones should look clean and grey/white.

  2. The Long Boil

    • Return the clean bones (and fatback, if using) to the clean pot.
    • Fill with fresh cold water to cover the bones by at least 2 inches.
    • Bring to a high boil. Maintain a rolling boil for 10 to 12 hours.
    • Note: Unlike clear stocks where you simmer gently, Tonkotsu requires agitation to mix the fat
      and water. Keep the lid on to minimize evaporation, but check every hour to top up with
      water so the bones stay submerged.

  3. Add Aromatics

    About 1 hour before you finish boiling (around the 11th hour), add the onion, garlic, and
    ginger. Adding them too early can make the broth taste stewed or "muddy."

  4. Make the Tare

    While the broth boils, combine the soy sauce, mirin, salt, and kombu in a small saucepan. Bring to
    a simmer, let it steep for 5 minutes, then remove the kombu. Set aside.

  5. Strain the Broth

    • Once the broth is thick, milky, and sticky (your lips should stick together slightly when tasting
      it), strain it through a fine-mesh sieve into a clean pot.
    • Discard the solids (the bones should be brittle and hollow).

  6. Cook the Noodles

    • Bring a separate pot of water to a boil.
    • Cook the ramen noodles according to the package instructions (usually 1–2 minutes for
      fresh noodles). Drain well.

  7. Assembly

    • Tare First: Ladle 2-3 tablespoons of Tare into the bottom of your serving bowl.
    • Broth Second: Pour the piping hot pork broth over the Tare.
    • Noodles Thrid: Add the noodles and use chopsticks to lift and fold them so they look neat.
    • Toppings: Arrange the Chashu, egg, mushrooms, and scallions on top. Serve immediately.
